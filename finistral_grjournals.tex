\begin{grabstract}
We present Finistral-7B-LoRA, a parameter-efficient financial-sentiment classifier produced by Low-Rank Adapting the 7-billion-parameter Mistral-7B-v0.1 language model. The adapter is trained on the open-source FinGPT/fingpt-sentiment-train corpus comprising approximately 77,000 labelled sentences drawn from press releases, wire headlines, analyst commentary, and Reddit finance threads. Training is conducted on Indiana University's Big Red 200 supercomputer using two NVIDIA A100 (40\,GB) GPUs, completing in under 120 minutes while updating only $\approx$\,0.2\,\% of the model parameters. On the Financial PhraseBank benchmark (sentences\_allagree split), Finistral-7B-LoRA achieves 99.56\,\% accuracy and a weighted F1 score of 0.996, reducing test errors to just ten instances and outperforming five strong FinGPT baselines as well as FinBERT by substantial margins. We further demonstrate that the full-precision adapter occupies only 83.9\,MB and that inference latency remains below 100\,ms on both GPU and CPU, making the model practical for real-time financial news monitoring. The released repository Ayansk11/Finistral-7B\_lora on Hugging Face contains the full-precision adapter, a 4-bit GGML variant, tokenizer files, and a Gradio-based inference interface for reproducible research.
\end{grabstract}

\grkeywords{financial sentiment analysis, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT), Mistral-7B, large language models, FinGPT, Financial PhraseBank}

\section{Introduction}

Financial markets digest a relentless stream of earnings releases, macro-economic announcements, social-media chatter, and regulatory filings. Traders and risk managers depend on sentiment analysis systems that can transform this unstructured text into structured signals---typically the triplet positive, neutral, or negative---within seconds. Conventional rule-based lexicons \cite{loughran2011} and classical machine-learning pipelines capture obvious polarity cues, yet they struggle with the domain's specialised jargon (``EPS miss''), pragmatic hedges (``guidance was raised but remains below expectations''), and rapidly evolving slang from forums such as WallStreetBets.

Large language models (LLMs) have recently set new performance bars on general-domain sentiment tasks, but two obstacles limit their usefulness in finance:

\textbf{Domain mismatch:} Out-of-the-box LLMs misinterpret sector-specific expressions and implicit market context.

\textbf{Compute cost:} Full fine-tuning of multi-billion-parameter models demands prohibitive GPU hours and storage, placing them out of reach for most academic or boutique-fund settings.

Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) \cite{hu2022} address both issues by inserting small trainable matrices into each transformer layer while freezing the backbone. This yields two attractive properties: (i) the modified model can be trained on a single commodity GPU, and (ii) the resulting adapter file is lightweight, making deployment trivial.

\subsection{Research Questions}

I investigate whether a LoRA-based adaptation of the compute-efficient Mistral-7B decoder can provide state-of-the-art sentiment classification in finance:

\textbf{Research Question 1:} Can a LoRA-tuned Mistral-7B surpass specialised encoders such as FinBERT and the latest FinGPT adapters on the Financial PhraseBank benchmark?

\textbf{Research Question 2:} Does the PEFT recipe retain training- and inference-time efficiency suitable for realistic academic or start-up budgets?

\subsection{Our Approach}

To answer these questions, I built Finistral-7B-LoRA. The adapter is trained on the publicly available FinGPT/fingpt-sentiment-train corpus (${\sim}$77\,k labelled sentences) and validated on a 5\,\% hold-out split. Training runs for four epochs on two A100 (40\,GB) GPUs of Indiana University's Big Red 200 supercomputer, completing in under 120 minutes while updating only $\approx$\,0.2\,\% of the model parameters. Validation loss bottomed out at 0.1008 after epoch 2 and began to rise thereafter, so that checkpoint is used for all downstream evaluations.

\subsection{Contributions}

My work makes three key contributions:

\textbf{Model release:} I publish Finistral-7B-LoRA along with full-precision (83.9\,MB) and 4-bit GGML (168\,MB) weights, tokeniser files, and inference scripts.

\textbf{State-of-the-art performance:} The adapter achieves 99.56\,\% accuracy and 0.996 weighted F1 on Financial PhraseBank (sentences\_allagree), reducing test errors to just ten instances and outperforming five strong FinGPT baselines by large margins.

\textbf{Efficiency analysis:} We demonstrate that high-quality financial sentiment models can be trained in $<$\,1 GPU-hour per epoch and deployed with minimal memory overhead, opening the door to low-cost, reproducible FinNLP research.


\section{Related Work}

Early attempts at financial sentiment analysis relied on domain-specific lexicons such as the Loughran--McDonald word lists \cite{loughran2011}, which map accounting terms to positive or negative polarity. Although lexicon rules are transparent, their coverage is limited and they struggle with figurative or contextual language. Subsequent classical machine-learning pipelines trained support-vector or recurrent networks on Financial PhraseBank \cite{malo2014}---a 4.8\,k-sentence news corpus with crowd-sourced sentiment labels---achieving modest gains but still underperforming human readers.

The advent of pre-trained language models (PLMs) led to a sharp performance jump. BERT \cite{devlin2019} introduced deep bidirectional pre-training for language understanding, and FinBERT \cite{araci2019} fine-tunes BERT on a large corpus of SEC filings, significantly outperforming earlier feature-engineering baselines on both opinion mining and aspect sentiment tasks. FinBERT, however, requires updating the full 110\,M parameters and thus incurs substantial GPU cost for domain shifts.

To reduce compute requirements, the community has embraced Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT). LoRA \cite{hu2022} freezes backbone weights and injects small rank-decomposition matrices, cutting trainable parameters by orders of magnitude while matching full fine-tuning quality. QLoRA \cite{dettmers2023} extends this with 4-bit quantisation, enabling fine-tuning of 65B-parameter models on a single 48\,GB GPU. Surveys \cite{peftsurvey2024} catalogue dozens of PEFT variants---including adapters, prefix-tuning---and highlight their importance for resource-constrained practitioners. Zhang et al.\ \cite{zhang2023instruct} demonstrate that instruction tuning general-purpose LLMs on financial sentiment data yields strong performance, particularly where numerical understanding and contextual comprehension are vital.

Within finance, the open-source FinGPT project \cite{yang2023}\footnote{Models and datasets available at \url{https://huggingface.co/FinGPT}} provides data-centric pipelines and a zoo of LoRA adapters for Llama-, Falcon- and Bloom-based models. These checkpoints form strong baselines in the annual FinNLP shared tasks \cite{finnlp2023}, which benchmark sentiment, headline classification and market-impact prediction. Yet, published FinGPT adapters still lag behind specialised encoders like FinBERT on the Financial PhraseBank test split.

Parallel to algorithmic advances, model architectures have evolved toward compute efficiency. The recently released Mistral-7B \cite{mistral2023} employs sliding-window attention and grouped query projection to halve inference cost while retaining GPT-3-level accuracy. Combining Mistral's lean decoder with LoRA thus promises a sweet-spot of speed and accuracy that prior financial studies have not fully explored.

Our work situates itself at this intersection: we leverage the Mistral-7B backbone and the FinGPT sentiment corpus, apply LoRA for parameter-efficient adaptation, and report state-of-the-art results on Financial PhraseBank, surpassing both FinBERT and existing FinGPT adapters.


\section{Problem Definition and Dataset}

\subsection{Task Formulation}

Given a single English news headline or short text snippet $x$, predict a sentiment label $y \in \{\text{negative}, \text{neutral}, \text{positive}\}$. Performance is evaluated with Accuracy and Weighted F1---the same metrics adopted by FinNLP shared-task benchmarks.

\subsection{Training Corpus}

I fine-tuned on the open-source FinGPT/fingpt-sentiment-train dataset \cite{yang2023}. After deduplication the corpus contains 76,772 sentences gathered from press releases, wire headlines, analyst commentary, and Reddit finance threads.

\subsection{Preprocessing}

\textbf{Pipeline Cleaning} -- Unicode-normalise, remove HTML artefacts, collapse whitespace.

\textbf{Tokenisation:} Use the native \textit{Mistral-7B-v0.1} 32\,k BPE tokeniser.

\textbf{Prompt templating:} Each sentence is wrapped exactly as in the training script:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Instruction: What is the sentiment of this news? Please choose an answer from \{negative/neutral/positive\}}

\textit{Input: \{text\}}

\textit{Answer:}
\end{quote}

This ``instruction-style'' format consistently triggers single-token answers that simplify label mapping.

\textbf{Label Mapping:} Generation is truncated at the first decoded token; a regex maps negative $\rightarrow$ 0, neutral $\rightarrow$ 1, positive $\rightarrow$ 2.

\section{Methodology}

This section explains how I adapt the 7-billion-parameter \textit{Mistral-7B-v0.1} language model to three-way financial sentiment classification with minimal compute and memory overhead.

\subsection{Base Model}

\textit{Mistral-7B-v0.1} is a decoder-only Transformer that combines grouped-query attention and sliding-window positional encodings to halve inference cost compared with comparably sized GPT-style models \cite{mistral2023}. We load the backbone in 8-bit NF4 format (\texttt{load\_in\_8bit=True} in Transformers) so that a single 40\,GB A100 can accommodate both weights and optimiser states.

\subsection{LoRA Adaptation}

I employed \textbf{Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)} \cite{hu2022} to inject trainable rank-decomposition matrices into every attention-projection and feed-forward linear layer while leaving the original weights frozen.

\begin{table}[htbp]

A second adapter file (\texttt{ggml-adapter-model.bin}, 168\,MB) is produced via 4-bit weight quantisation for CPU/edge deployments.

I used the Hugging Face Trainer API with the following settings.

\section{Result Analysis \& Discussion}

\subsection{Quantitative Gains}

Finistral-7B-LoRA delivers \textbf{state-of-the-art performance} on \textit{Financial PhraseBank dataset}:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{+69 F1} over the zero-shot Mistral-7B backbone
  \item \textbf{+78--96 F1} over every FinGPT LoRA baseline (Llama2, Llama3-8B, Falcon-7B, Bloom-7B1)

The adapter reduces test errors from $>$\,1,400 (Mistral-7B base) to \textbf{10}.
All three classes are handled evenly: Negative (98.7\,\% F1), Neutral (99.4\,\%), Positive (99.3\,\%).

\subsection{Limitations \& Ethical Considerations}

\textbf{Label noise:} FinGPT relies on distant supervision; some training sentences may be mislabelled, potentially propagating bias.

\textbf{Domain scope:} The model is English-only and tuned on equity-centric text; performance on commodities or multilingual filings is untested.

\textbf{Market impact:} Releasing highly accurate sentiment models could amplify herding behaviour if widely adopted.

\textbf{Overconfidence:} Near-perfect headline accuracy does not imply robustness to longer documents or adversarial phrasing; calibration techniques should be explored.

\subsection{Key Takeaways}

LoRA + domain data + prompt engineering $\Rightarrow$ near-perfect accuracy with negligible compute.

Training beyond the validation-loss trough yields no benefit and can harm generalisation.

Remaining errors cluster around sarcasm and conditional language---prime targets for future dataset curation or multi-label extensions.


\section{Conclusion}

Finistral-7B-LoRA demonstrates that \textbf{state-of-the-art financial sentiment accuracy (99.6\,\% / 0.996 F1) can be reached with only 0.2\,\% trainable parameters, one-node GPU time, and an 84\,MB adapter file}. By combining the compute-efficient Mistral-7B backbone with Low-Rank Adaptation and the FinGPT sentiment corpus, we surpass both full-model FinBERT fine-tuning and a suite of FinGPT LoRA baselines while staying within academic compute budgets on Indiana University's Big Red 200 supercomputer. Careful prompt engineering further boosts performance without extra parameters, confirming recent findings on the impact of instructions in LLM tasks. The project validates the broader PEFT vision that small, targeted updates can rival or exceed heavy full fine-tuning approaches.


\section{Future Work}

\textbf{Streaming EDGAR \& real-time news:} Implement continual learning to keep the model current as market language evolves, aligning with FinNLP shared-task goals.

\textbf{Human-in-the-loop curation:} Use active learning loops to clean noisy FinGPT labels and focus annotation on sarcasm or mixed-tone edge cases revealed in our error analysis.

\textbf{4-bit \& QLoRA fusion:} Combine LoRA with GGML/QLoRA \cite{dettmers2023} quantisation for sub-100\,MB end-to-end models deployable on laptops and edge devices.

\textbf{GRPO-based reasoning integration:} Apply Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) \cite{shao2024deepseekmath} to inject chain-of-thought reasoning into Finistral-7B-LoRA, enabling the model to articulate its sentiment rationale rather than returning a bare label. GRPO eliminates the need for a separate value model, making reinforcement-learning-based alignment feasible within academic compute budgets.

\textbf{On-device small language models:} Distil the reasoning-augmented adapter into sub-3B parameter small language models (SLMs) optimised for on-device inference on consumer hardware such as iPhone 15, Android smartphones, and low-end laptops. Combining GRPO-trained reasoning with aggressive quantisation (4-bit/2-bit) and architecture pruning could yield models that run entirely on-device with sub-200\,ms latency, eliminating cloud dependency and preserving user privacy for personal finance applications.
